Most teams think they understand prompt chaining until they have to teach it.

I built an interactive platform that teaches 7 chaining patterns through gamified space missions—not slides, not docs, but drag-and-drop puzzles where you're "Mission Control routing deep-space signals."

The surprising part? The learning didn't come from better explanations. It came from role-play. When you're placing operations in sequence to decrypt incoming Artemis telemetry, your brain doesn't engage the same way it does reading "A → B → C." The narrative frame changed everything.

This revealed something bigger: we're teaching technical concepts upside down. We explain first, hope understanding follows. But understanding comes from feeling competent, and competence comes from doing, not reading.

If most AI engineers can't articulate the difference between branching and recursive chaining, is the problem their intelligence or our teaching method?
