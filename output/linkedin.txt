I built a space mission game to teach prompt chaining, and something unexpected happened: people understood it.

The problem isn't the concept. It's that documentation treats patterns like math—abstract, universal. Sequential chaining is "A → B → C." Branching is "A → [B₁, B₂] → C." True, but invisible.

So I reframed it: Artemis probe sends corrupted telemetry. Decode it through seven stages or lose the signal forever. Same constraint. Same logic. But now it *feels* like something matters.

The moment it clicked: watching someone toggle between technical mode and mission mode. The exact same process. Different language. Suddenly they said, "Oh—it's the same thing either way." That's when learning happens.

Most tech education strips away context to seem rigorous, then wonders why students can't apply what they learned. Here's the insight: humans internalize patterns from *stakes*, not descriptions. From narratives where failure is costly. From scenarios that demand we arrange concepts in the right order or lose something we value.

When you teach a technical skill, how much of your job is documentation versus experience design?
